## The seductive idea

You have two tasks on the same image: segment the tumor, and classify it. The
textbook move is multi-task learning: share one encoder, grow two heads, and let
the shared representation help both tasks. It is elegant, it is efficient, and on
the BRISC 2025 dataset it was the wrong call.

This post is about the experiment that talked me out of it.

## The setup

The segmentation model is a five-level U-Net trained under a combined Dice-BCE
loss. The classifier is a DenseNet-121. Trained separately, they are strong:

| Task | Metric | Score |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Segmentation | Test Dice | 88.22% |
| Segmentation | mIoU | 79.74% |
| Classification | Accuracy | 97.50% |

Then I built a joint model: one shared encoder, a segmentation decoder, and a
classification head, trained with a summed loss.

$$
\mathcal{L}_{joint} = \lambda_{seg}\,\mathcal{L}_{seg} + \lambda_{cls}\,\mathcal{L}_{cls}
$$

## The result

The joint model lost on both tasks:

| Approach | Segmentation (Dice) | Classification (Acc) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Separate | 83.10% (val) | 97.50% |
| Joint | 79.02% | 91.69% |
| Difference | -4.91% | -5.81% |

Nearly six points of classification accuracy, gone. Sharing hurt.

## Why it failed here

Three reasons, in order of how much I think they mattered:

1. **Task interference.** Segmentation wants spatially precise, high-resolution
   features; classification wants globally pooled, invariant ones. Forcing one
   encoder to serve both pulls the representation in two directions.
2. **Loss balancing.** The `lambda` weights are a hyperparameter you now have to
   tune, and getting them wrong quietly starves one task.
3. **Dataset scale.** Multi-task learning tends to pay off when data is
   plentiful enough to learn a genuinely shared structure. With a few thousand
   image-mask pairs, there was not enough signal to find one.

## The lesson

Multi-task learning is not free. It trades a modeling constraint (shared
features must serve every task) for a parameter saving. That trade is worth it
when the tasks are aligned and data is abundant. When the tasks pull apart and
the dataset is modest, two focused models win.

The unglamorous recommendation I shipped: **train them separately.** U-Net for
segmentation, DenseNet-121 for classification. It is less clever and it works
better, which is usually the right trade.
