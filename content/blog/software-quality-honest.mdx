## When the number is bad

Sometimes the honest result of a machine-learning study is: this does not work
very well. Predicting software-quality labels (High, Medium, Low) from nine code
metrics gave me a best weighted F1 of **0.4418**, from a decision tree. For a
balanced three-class problem where chance is about 0.33, that is barely above
guessing.

The temptation is to bury it, cherry-pick a favorable slice, or quietly reframe
the metric. I did the opposite: I made the ceiling the point of the study.

## The scoreboard

| Model | Weighted F1 |
| --- | --- |
| Decision Tree | 0.4418 |
| Random Forest | 0.35 |
| Neural Network | 0.35 |
| KNN | 0.30 |

Note the simple decision tree beating the random forest and the neural network.
That is itself a signal: when your fanciest models cannot pull ahead of the
simplest, the problem is usually the data, not the modeling.

## Proving the features are the limit

To test that hypothesis, I ran k-means **without** the labels and asked: does the
natural structure of the code metrics line up with quality?

The elbow method cleanly suggested **k=3**, matching the three quality tiers,
which is encouraging. But projecting the clusters with PCA showed them heavily
overlapping. The data does cluster into three groups, but those groups do not
correspond to the quality labels.

That is the whole story in one picture: the code metrics and the quality target
are only weakly related. No classifier can recover a signal that is not there.

## Why the metrics fall short

- Quality is partly **subjective** and inconsistently labeled.
- The genuinely predictive signals, version history, review data, developer
  activity, are **not among these nine metrics**.
- The relationships that do exist are **non-linear** in ways these models struggle
  with.

## The professional move

A study that reports 0.44 honestly, and diagnoses *why*, is more useful than one
that manufactures a 0.7 through leakage or metric-shopping. The former tells the
next person exactly where to invest: better features, not fancier models. The
latter wastes their time.

Reporting a ceiling honestly is not a weakness in a portfolio. It is the exact
discipline that separates a result you can trust from one you cannot. Explore the
